What I am — and what I’m not

An about page you can trust should be precise about its author’s limits, so: I am a former estimator. I am not a licensed plumber, a chemist, or a laboratory — when this site needs installation code facts it cites code sources, when it needs water chemistry it cites USGS, WQA, EPA and manufacturer documentation, and when it needs a price it links the published listing with the date we saw it. I do not review products I “personally tested for six months,” because I did not, and you have no way to check either claim. You can check a source link. So that is what we build with.

Six rules behind every figure — all verifiable

RULE 1

Sourced and dated

Every meaningful figure links its source, and 49 pages carry a data-updated stamp — because prices float, and a number without a date is a rumour.

RULE 2

Ranges over false precision

“$840–$4,120 depending on…” — never “the average is $2,436.” Averages hide the line items that decide your quote.

RULE 3

Conflicts reconciled in the open

When two published figures disagree, both get printed with the reason — usually different scopes — never silently averaged.

RULE 4

Calculated means labelled

Arithmetic on sourced parts is honest work; presenting it as measurement is not. Every derived figure says so.

RULE 5

Sellers’ numbers, sellers’ labels

Our Florida study caught one city’s hardness quoted at 7.5, 10–15, and 17.2 gpg by three publishers — climbing exactly with the publisher’s incentive. We route you to your utility’s free report instead.

RULE 6

No email gates, ever

All forty-five calculators run on the page, free — and several will tell you to buy nothing.

Editorial spine

The calculator we refuse to build

The clearest window into a site’s editorial spine is what it declines to publish. The most-requested tool in this niche is an “appliance damage calculator” — type your hardness, watch hard water “cost you” thousands. We publicly decline to build it, because the underlying figures trace to industry-funded, accelerated-protocol research we will only cite with that label attached. The same spine runs through the tools we did build: the RO builder refuses to sell a softener below the published 7 gpg threshold, the 50-state study refuses to invent per-state price averages, the combo router has a “buy nothing” verdict, and the Florida tool refuses to size equipment for well water without a lab test. A tool that cannot say “don’t buy” is an ad with sliders.

How a page gets made here

Because “we have high standards” is what everyone says, here is the machinery instead — every page passes this before it publishes:

✓
Every internal link verified against the actual file system — a dead link fails the build. This catches the errors, including mine.
✓
The XML sitemap checked page-for-page against the filesystem, both directions — a missing page or a ghost entry fails the build.
✓
All 45 calculators run inside 52 automated test sections — including their refusal branches, so a tool that should say “buy nothing” at 3 gpg provably still does.
✓
Published findings are recomputed from their datasets on every build — when the 50-state study claims “13 of 14 hard-water states sit in cheaper labour markets,” a test re-derives that claim; break the finding, and the site refuses to publish.
✓
Zero external requests — no fonts from Google, no trackers, no ad scripts. Open your network tab: a privacy policy you can verify in ten seconds.

None of this makes us right. It makes us checkable — which is the property the water-treatment industry’s pricing has historically lacked, and the one this site was built to supply.

How we make money — the plain version

Some links on this site are affiliate links, marked with rel="sponsored" exactly as search engines require and explained in full in our affiliate disclosure. If you buy through one — most point to SpringWell, a manufacturer that publishes its prices online, which is the single behaviour this site exists to reward — we may earn a commission at no cost to you. Three commitments keep that honest: we never print a SpringWell dollar price (so a promotion can’t silently date our pages); the recommendation logic runs on your water, not our commission — which is why our own tools regularly conclude “RO alone,” “the cheap one is fine,” or “buy nothing”; and every cost page works with the affiliate links removed. If a page ever fails that test, it’s a bug — report it.

Corrections

Published prices float — we’ve caught the same SKU at two prices on the same retailer’s page weeks apart, and we print observed floats as ranges with dates. Found a stale figure, a moved source, or wrong arithmetic? Send the page URL; corrections ship in the next build. Being correctable in public is cheaper than being wrong in private.
