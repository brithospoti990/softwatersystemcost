I priced these systems for 15 years. Now I publish the numbers.

I’m Robert Miller. For more than fifteen years I worked around residential plumbing and water-treatment installations in the Daytona Beach area — usually as the person turning a walkthrough of a garage, a plumbing layout, and a water complaint into the number on the quote. I was not the one soldering the pipe; I was the one deciding what the pipe, the labour, the permit and the tank should cost, and explaining that number when a homeowner asked. Pricing the work was the job.

That is the entire premise of this site: the estimating worksheet, opened for the homeowner instead of against them.

What I am — and what I’m not

An about page you can trust should be precise about its author’s limits, so: I am a former estimator. I am not a licensed plumber, a chemist, or a laboratory — when this site needs installation code facts it cites code sources, when it needs water chemistry it cites USGS, WQA, EPA and manufacturer documentation, and when it needs a price it links the published listing with the date we saw it. I do not review products I “personally tested for six months,” because I did not, and you have no way to check either claim. You can check a source link. So that is what we build with.

How this site works: six rules, all verifiable

1. Every meaningful figure is sourced and dated. As of this update the site carries 370 outbound source links across 121 domains — utility reports, USGS, BLS, county permit schedules, retailer listings — and 49 pages carry a “data updated” stamp, because prices float and a number without a date is a rumour. 2. Ranges over false precision. You will find “$840–$4,120 depending on…” here, not “the average is $2,436,” because averages hide the line items that decide your quote. 3. Conflicting sources are reconciled in the open. When two published figures disagree, both are printed with the reason (usually: they describe different scopes) — never silently averaged. 4. Calculated numbers are labelled calculated. Arithmetic on sourced parts is honest work; presenting it as measurement is not. 5. Sellers’ numbers are treated as sellers’ numbers. Our Florida study caught one city’s hardness quoted at 7.5, 10–15, and 17.2 gpg by three publishers — climbing in exact order of the publisher’s incentive. We route you to your utility’s free report instead. 6. No email gates, no PDF ransoms. All forty-five calculators run on the page, free, and several of them will tell you to buy nothing.

The calculator we refuse to build

The clearest window into a site’s editorial spine is what it declines to publish. The most-requested tool in this niche is an “appliance damage calculator” — type your hardness, watch hard water “cost you” thousands. We publicly decline to build it, because the underlying figures trace to industry-funded, accelerated-protocol research that we’re only willing to cite with that label attached. The same spine runs through the tools we did build: the RO builder refuses to sell a softener below the published 7 gpg threshold, the 50-state study refuses to invent per-state price averages, the combo router has a “buy nothing” verdict, and the Florida tool refuses to size equipment for well water without a lab test. A tool that cannot say “don’t buy” is an ad with sliders.

How a page gets made here

Because “we have high standards” is what everyone says, here is the machinery instead. Every page on this site passes an automated check suite before it publishes: every internal link is verified against the actual file system (a dead link fails the build — this catches the errors, including mine), every FAQ is length-checked for search compliance, the XML sitemap is verified page-for-page against the actual filesystem in both directions, every affiliate link is verified to carry its required disclosure attributes, and all forty-five calculators run inside 52 automated test sections that assert their arithmetic against the sourced figures — including their refusal branches, so a tool that’s supposed to say “buy nothing” at 3 gpg provably still does. When our 50-state study claims “13 of 14 hard-water states sit in cheaper labour markets,” a test recomputes that claim from the dataset on every build; if a future edit breaks the finding, the site refuses to publish. The site also loads no external fonts, trackers from ad networks, or third-party scripts — check the network tab; zero outside requests is a privacy policy you can verify in ten seconds. None of this makes us right. It makes us checkable, which is the property the water-treatment industry’s pricing has historically lacked — and the one this site was built to supply.

How we make money — the plain version

Some links on this site are affiliate links, marked with rel="sponsored" exactly as search engines require. If you buy through one — most point to SpringWell, a manufacturer that publishes its prices online, which is the single behaviour this site exists to reward — we may earn a commission at no cost to you. Three commitments keep that honest: we never print a SpringWell dollar price (so a promotion can’t silently date our pages — check the current price yourself); the recommendation logic runs on your water, not our commission — which is why our own tools regularly conclude “RO alone,” “the cheap one is fine,” or “buy nothing”; and every cost page works with the affiliate links removed. If a page ever fails that test, it’s a bug — report it.

Corrections

Published prices float — we’ve caught the same SKU at two prices on the same retailer’s page weeks apart, and we print observed floats as ranges with dates. If you find a figure that’s gone stale, a source that’s moved, or arithmetic that’s simply wrong, write to the address below with the page URL; corrections ship in the next site build and material ones are noted on the page. Being correctable in public is cheaper than being wrong in private.
